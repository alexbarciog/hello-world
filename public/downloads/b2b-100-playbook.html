Chapter One

Why Most B2B Companies
Never Hit 100 Customers

The three traps that stall growth before it starts

Here's a counterintuitive truth: the 0–100 customer stage has almost nothing to do with your product. Companies with average products hit 100. Companies with exceptional products stall at 12. The difference comes down to three traps — and almost every early-stage B2B founder falls into at least one of them.

Trap 1 — Targeting people who aren't ready to buy

Most founders build a list and work it. They export from Apollo or LinkedIn, craft a sequence, and start reaching out to anyone who fits their ICP. The problem isn't the list. It's the timing.

A company that would be a perfect customer in six months is worthless to you today. They're not thinking about your problem. They're not searching for solutions. Your message lands in a brain with zero room for it — and gets deleted in under a second.

The cold email industry average reply rate is 1.8%. For every 100 emails sent, 98 people ignored you. The fix isn't better copy. It's targeting people who are actively looking right now.

Trap 2 — Pitching before trust exists

You get someone interested. You've got 30 minutes. You want to use them well — so you walk through the product. Features, pricing, case studies. The whole thing. They nod, say "interesting, I'll think about it," and disappear.

Not because the product was bad. Because you pitched before you had permission. Trust comes from demonstrated value, not claimed value. The fastest path to trust is giving something genuinely useful before you ask for anything in return.

Trap 3 — Waiting for referrals

You close your first few clients. The product works. They like you. So you wait. Surely they'll tell people. Surely word will spread.

It doesn't. Not automatically. People are busy and they don't think about your growth. Referrals at scale require a system, an incentive, and an explicit ask. We'll cover that in Chapter 7.

The companies that hit 100 customers aren't smarter or better funded. They figured out that timing beats targeting — showing up when someone is actively looking is 40× more powerful than the right message at the wrong moment.

Chapter Three

Where Your Next 100
Customers Already Are

They're not in a database. They're in a conversation.

Your next 100 customers are not waiting passively for you to find them. They're actively talking. Posting questions. Asking for recommendations. Venting about their current tools. And they're doing it in public, right now, on LinkedIn, Reddit, and X — while almost no one is watching.

LinkedIn

B2B buyers post professionally about their problems. "Does anyone have experience with [category]?" They announce they're "exploring options." They comment "this is exactly what we're dealing with" on competitor content. They post job descriptions that signal they're scaling a function — which means they need tools to support it. Every one of these is a buying signal.

Reddit

Reddit is where buyers go for honest, unfiltered advice. "What are you actually using for [problem]?" and "We're evaluating [category], what should we know?" The intent is extremely high — people are there specifically to get answers to real problems, not to network. And they're brutally honest about what they need.

X (Twitter)

Frustrated buyers vent publicly on X. "Is there a tool that does X without Y?" and "Why is every [category] tool so bad at [specific thing]?" Someone frustrated enough with their current solution to tweet about it is actively looking for an alternative. That's your moment.

What buying signals look like in the wild
Anyone know a good tool for finding warm leads without blasting cold emails? Our reply rates are abysmal.
We're evaluating outbound tools — would love recommendations from people who've actually used them.
Just switched away from [competitor]. The old approach isn't working. What's actually working for agencies in 2025?
Our team needs a better lead gen system. Tired of cold email. Anyone doing something different?
Hiring a Head of Sales next month — need to get our outbound system in order before they start.

The timing problem

These signals have a half-life of roughly 4 hours. A post asking for tool recommendations on LinkedIn gets 3–8 responses in the first hour from whoever is already watching. After 24 hours, the poster has started evaluating the first two or three options they received. After 72 hours, they may have already made a decision.

If you find that post three days later during a manual search, you're not late — you're invisible. The opportunity is completely gone. This is why systematically monitoring these platforms in real time is one of the highest-leverage activities a B2B founder can do at this stage.

Chapter Four

The Signal-First
Outreach System

How to reach the right person at the right moment

Signal-first outreach is the opposite of traditional outbound. Instead of building a list of people who might be interested and hoping your timing is right, you identify people who are already interested right now and show up in that conversation.

Step 1 — Define your signal universe

A buying signal is any public post indicating someone is actively in-market for what you sell. But most companies get this wrong — they think in analyst language: "B2B lead generation," "outbound optimisation." Your buyers don't talk like that.

Build your signal list from the language your buyers actually use when they're frustrated, searching, or switching. That's what gets caught. That's what converts.

  • "Does anyone know a good [category] tool that actually works?"
  • "We're switching away from [competitor] — what are you using?"
  • "Our [process] is broken. Looking for recommendations."
  • "Hiring a [role] next quarter" — signals they're scaling and need your service
  • "Just had a terrible experience with [current tool]" — they're actively looking

Step 2 — Lead with value, always

When you find a signal, your instinct will be to reply with your product. Don't. The fastest way to kill a warm opportunity is to turn it into a sales pitch the moment you show up. Answer the question genuinely. Give them something useful first. The product mention comes naturally from that foundation.

Step 3 — The free sample approach

The single most effective first move in B2B outreach at this stage: offer something valuable for free, before they've paid you anything. It completely inverts the traditional sales dynamic. You're not asking for anything — you're proving value first.

Day 0
Hey [Name] — saw your post about [problem]. I actually have some relevant examples/data/leads that might be useful. Want me to send them over? No pitch — just thought it might help.
Day 1
Here they are: [the valuable thing]. This is essentially what our tool finds automatically. Happy to show you how it works — here's a quick walkthrough: [Loom/video]. No pressure.
Day 3
Hey — just checking if that was useful. If you want to try [your tool] properly, I'm happy to set you up. And if the timing isn't right, no worries at all.
Day 7
Last message from me — if the timing isn't right, totally fine. I'll leave you to it. If you ever want to revisit, you know where to find me. Good luck with [their goal].

Chapter Five

Selling Without
Sales Calls

Close customers asynchronously — while you sleep

At the 0–100 stage, your time is the scarcest resource you have. Every hour on a demo call is an hour not spent building, writing, or finding new customers. The math only works if you can close some customers asynchronously.

A sales process that requires your personal presence for every conversion caps your growth at the number of hours you can spend on calls per week. An async process runs 24/7.

The three assets you need

Asset 1: A Loom video (8–12 minutes)

Not a features walkthrough. A story. Start with the problem your customer had. Show your product solving it live, with real results. End with a single CTA: a direct link to sign up or start a trial. No call booking. No form. Direct to action.

Asset 2: One case study per major niche

One page. Three sections: the problem before, what they did, the specific result. Real numbers only. "Booked 3 meetings in 48 hours" beats "significantly improved pipeline velocity" in every test. Specificity builds trust. Vagueness destroys it.

Asset 3: A free sample of your product's value

Whatever your product does — give a taste of it for free before asking for payment. This is your most powerful trust-building move. It removes the biggest friction point in early B2B sales: the risk of paying before seeing proof.

The async sequence in practice

Someone engages with your content or responds to your outreach. Instead of booking a call, you send: the free sample → the Loom → the relevant case study → a direct link. That's the full sales process. At the $97–$500/month price point, a well-designed async process closes 20–35% of warm leads without a single call.

The rule: Only offer a call to someone who has already watched the Loom, read a case study, and is asking specific questions that can't be answered in writing. Everyone else gets the async sequence first.

Chapter Six

Content That Brings
Customers to You

One post a month can change your trajectory

Most founders treat content as a chore — something they do when they have time, when they feel inspired, when they have something to say. That produces inconsistency, and inconsistency is the death of content as a growth channel.

Content that drives B2B customer acquisition follows different rules. It's not about reach. It's about relevance. One post seen by 500 of your exact ICP is worth more than a viral post seen by 50,000 people who'll never buy from you.

The three post types that drive growth

Type 1: The Result Post

Lead with a specific, surprising number. Every element earns its place: the number, the timeframe, the contrast with the status quo. End with a low-friction CTA that forces engagement: "Comment [WORD] and I'll send you [valuable thing]." Engagement boosts reach and gives you a list of warm leads to follow up with in DMs.

Type 2: The Contrarian Post

Take a position your ICP secretly believes but hasn't seen stated clearly. These posts get shared because people want to show them to colleagues. The format: make a specific, slightly uncomfortable claim about why the conventional approach fails — then reveal the better way.

Type 3: The Founder Truth Post

Once a month, share your real numbers. Customers, MRR, what worked, what failed, what surprised you. Don't polish it. Founders building in public and sharing real failures get 3–10× the reach of founders who only share wins. People root for honesty. And every person following your journey is a warm lead who already feels like they know you.

The Sunday Session — your most important habit

Block 2–3 hours every Sunday. Write all three posts for the coming week. Create one lead magnet. Outline your video or newsletter. Everything planned before Monday starts. You wake up Tuesday and post what you already wrote. There's no "I need to think of something to post today."

The weeks you skip this will show up in your customer count three weeks later. Guard this time like a board meeting.

Lead magnets that convert

A lead magnet works when it delivers immediate, specific value to your exact ICP — and when consuming it makes them want your product. Generic tip lists don't do this. The best lead magnets:

  • Give something so specific it feels almost unfair — a list, a framework, a template they can use today
  • Demonstrate your core value before they pay you anything
  • Are distributed via "comment to receive" — not a link — to maximise reach and warm leads
  • Pre-sell your product by making the reader think "I need a tool that does this automatically"

The founder who posts consistently for 90 days will out-grow the one who goes viral once and disappears. Consistency is the unfair advantage nobody talks about.

Chapter Seven

The Referral &
Affiliate Flywheel

Turn 3 customers into 30 without extra outreach

Your existing customers are not an end point. They're the beginning of your most powerful growth channel. Every paying customer is a node in a network. The question is whether you activate that network or leave it dormant.

The referral system

Referrals without a system are wishes. With a system they're a channel — measurable, repeatable, and growable. The system is three things:

  • A clear incentive. One free month per referral that converts. Not a discount — actual free time with your product. This costs you almost nothing and the referred customer pays for 11+ months minimum.
  • An explicit ask. Three days after signup, message every new customer personally: "Do you know 1–2 people who might get value from this? If they sign up, I'll give you a free month." Not a broadcast email. A real message.
  • A low-friction path. They shouldn't have to do more than forward a two-sentence message to a friend. Make the ask easy: "Could you just introduce us on LinkedIn?"

Warm referrals convert at 40–60%. One customer who sends you two referrals is worth more than 40 cold email leads.

The affiliate system

Affiliates are people who have your customers as their audience — consultants, coaches, newsletter creators, trainers. They don't use your product. They influence the people who do.

The offer: 30% recurring commission and a free account for life. One affiliate with 10,000 followers who mentions you once can send 5–15 signups — recurring revenue from a single post, forever.
